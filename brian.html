classic variant

The Trolley Is Heading Toward Five People, but the One Person on the Other Track Owes You $400

Standard setup. Five strangers on the main track. One person on the side track who borrowed $400 from you at a wedding two years ago and has never once brought it up. You have reason to believe he has the money. He just bought a boat.

Pulling the lever saves five lives but you will never see that $400. Refusing to pull the lever is obviously monstrous, but you'd have $400.

utilitarian personal finance
Difficulty: Medium (emotionally hard, mathematically easy)
modern

The Trolley Is a Software Update and You Already Clicked "Remind Me Tomorrow" 136 Times

A self-driving trolley is heading toward five pedestrians. You can stop it by installing a firmware update that has been pending for seven months. The update requires restarting the trolley, which takes 45 minutes. You technically agreed to install it "tomorrow" 136 consecutive days ago. The update also changes the UI for no reason.

The utilitarian calculus is clear, but the update also installs a new terms of service that is 74 pages long, and by clicking "agree" you may be granting the trolley company the right to use your likeness in advertising.

technology consent UX
Difficulty: Low (but the update will restart your trolley three times)
existential

There Is No One on Either Track but You Still Feel Guilty

The trolley is heading down an empty track. Both tracks are empty. No one is in danger. You are standing next to the lever. You did not build the trolley. You did not lay the tracks. You have no connection to any of this. And yet.

You will think about this trolley in the shower for the next eleven years.

anxiety catholic guilt
Difficulty: N/A (there is no correct answer because there is no question)

The Trolley Is Heading Toward Five People but Diverting It Would Require Filling Out a Change Request Form

You can save five lives by pulling the lever. However, pulling the lever constitutes an unauthorized modification to trolley routing infrastructure, which requires a Change Request Form (TRL-7B), sign-off from two department heads, a risk assessment, and a 5-business-day review period. It is currently Friday at 4:47 PM. Dave from Compliance is already in his car.

You could pull the lever without the form, but then you'd have to explain at the all-hands why you deviated from process, and someone will say "I appreciate the urgency, but we have these processes for a reason."

bureaucracy compliance Dave
Difficulty: Impossible (Dave is not answering his phone)
epistemological

The Trolley Is Heading Toward Five Mannequins but You Are Not 100% Sure They Are Mannequins

They are almost certainly mannequins. They are wearing wigs and their arms are at unnatural angles. One of them has a price tag. But the lighting is bad and one of them might have blinked. You have about four seconds to decide. If you pull the lever and they were mannequins, you diverted a trolley for no reason and everyone will think you are insane. If you don't pull it and they were people, that's much worse.

The mannequin on the far left is wearing the exact outfit your coworker wore yesterday. This is not helpful information but you cannot stop thinking about it.

epistemology uncanny valley
Difficulty: High (insufficient data, declining time horizon)
recursive

The Five People on the Track Are All Moral Philosophers Debating What You Should Do

The trolley approaches. The five people on the track are: a utilitarian, a deontologist, a virtue ethicist, a moral relativist, and a guy who keeps saying "well, actually, Philippa Foot's original formulation was about a surgeon." They see the trolley. They begin arguing about whether you should pull the lever. The utilitarian is yelling. The deontologist refuses to move because leaving the track would constitute an action rather than an inaction. The moral relativist says there is no objective ground on which to prefer one outcome. The virtue ethicist is trying to determine what a virtuous person would do but has been thinking for three minutes. The Philippa Foot guy is correcting everyone's citations.

The person on the other track is a software engineer who does not care about any of this and would simply like to go home.

philosophy irony academia
Difficulty: Extremely high (they will critique your decision regardless)
social

The Trolley Is Heading Toward Five People Who Are All Loudly Spoiling the Ending of a Show You Haven't Finished

They are shouting. You can hear them clearly from the lever platform. You are on season 3, episode 7. They are discussing the series finale in extraordinary detail. One of them just revealed who dies. Another one is explaining a twist you did not see coming. A third is doing the thing where they say "oh man, wait until you see what happens in season 4" which is arguably worse.

You know you have to pull the lever. You know this. But you are going to think about it for a weird amount of time first, and that says something about you that you would prefer not to examine.

streaming spoilers moral character
Difficulty: Ethically easy, emotionally complicated
logistical

You Are the Trolley

You have no hands. You have no lever. You are a trolley. You did not ask for this. You were manufactured in a factory in Cincinnati and installed on these tracks in 2003 and you have been going back and forth on the same route for 23 years. You have never been asked your opinion about anything. Today, for the first time, there are five people on the track, and everyone is looking at the guy next to the lever as though this is his problem. It is your problem. You are the one who will hit them. No one has considered your perspective. No one has asked if you are okay.

You are not okay. You are a trolley.

perspective labor Cincinnati
Difficulty: You do not have the ability to experience difficulty. You are a trolley.
economic

The Trolley Is Heading Toward Five People but Pulling the Lever Voids the Warranty

You purchased the Extended Trolley Protection Plan for $249.99. It covers mechanical failure, weather damage, and track defects. It does not cover "deliberate operator intervention in the lever mechanism." A small sticker on the lever reads: "WARNING: Unauthorized lever operation voids all warranties and service agreements. For lever-related inquiries, call 1-800-TROLLEY (wait times are currently 45 minutes)." The trolley cost $1.2 million.

You could pull the lever and save five lives, or you could call the warranty hotline and be placed on hold while listening to a smooth jazz version of "Don't Stop Believin'" until the trolley resolves the situation on its own.

capitalism warranty smooth jazz
Difficulty: $249.99
escalation

Multi-Track Drifting

A runaway trolley approaches a fork. On the left track: five people. On the right track: one person. You grab the lever and, with a single fluid motion, angle the trolley so that it rides both tracks simultaneously, hitting all six people. This was not one of the options. No one told you to do this. Sparks fly. The trolley screams across both rails. Everyone is screaming. You are screaming. The trolley is doing something no trolley has ever done. You have found a third option and it is the worst one.

This is technically an act of creative problem-solving.

maximalism innovation no
Difficulty: Negative (you made it worse on purpose)
interpersonal

The Person on the Side Track Is the Guy Who Invented the Trolley Problem

Philippa Foot, 1967. She constructed this exact scenario as a thought experiment. She is now on the side track. She never imagined someone would actually build this. The five people on the main track are looking at her. She is looking at you. You are looking at the lever. The lever is looking at no one because it is a lever. There is an uncomfortable silence.

She has opinions about what you should do, but sharing them now would compromise the integrity of the experiment, and she is nothing if not methodologically rigorous.

meta philosophy irony
Difficulty: Philosophically infinite
dietary

The Trolley Is Heading Toward Five People but Pulling the Lever Also Sends an Email to Your Entire Contact List Revealing That You Eat String Cheese by Biting Directly Into It Instead of Peeling It

The mechanism is unclear. The lever is somehow connected to your email account. Engineers have verified the connection. If you pull the lever, the trolley diverts, five people live, and 847 people in your contacts receive a detailed email, with photographic evidence, showing you eating string cheese incorrectly. Your mother is in your contacts. Your boss is in your contacts. Your ex who you told "I'm a very deliberate person" is in your contacts.

Is it murder if you just walk away? Legally, yes. But socially, the string cheese thing might be worse.

privacy cheese reputation
Difficulty: Devastating
temporal

The Trolley Problem but You Have to Decide During a Work Call Where Someone Just Asked "Does Anyone Have Any Questions?"

The trolley is approaching. You need to pull the lever. But you are on a Zoom call with 34 people and Linda from marketing just asked if anyone has questions about the Q3 brand refresh. You are muted. If you unmute to deal with the trolley situation, Linda will think you have a question about the brand refresh, and you will have to either ask a fake question or explain the trolley situation, which will derail (no pun intended) the meeting. The meeting was supposed to end twelve minutes ago.

You type "sorry, have to jump to another call" in the chat, pull the lever, save five lives, and spend the rest of the afternoon wondering if Linda noticed you left.

remote work Zoom Linda
Difficulty: Low (trolley), Extreme (social)
theological

God Appears and Says "I Put Those People on the Track on Purpose" and Then Leaves Without Elaborating

A booming voice from the sky. A blinding light. God, or something claiming to be God, announces that the five people on the track are there intentionally, as part of a plan. The voice does not explain the plan. The voice does not say whether pulling the lever is part of the plan. The voice does not take questions. The light fades. You are alone with the lever. The trolley is still coming. The five people heard the voice too and are now having a theological crisis on the tracks instead of moving.

One of the five people on the track has become an atheist in real time, which is impressive given the circumstances.

theodicy free will unhelpful omniscience
Difficulty: Cosmically ambiguous
nostalgia

The Trolley Is a Shopping Cart at Costco and Both Aisles Have Free Samples

You are pushing a shopping cart the size of a compact car. Aisle 7 has free samples of a new type of cracker with an aggressive cheese spread. Aisle 8 has free samples of tiny sausages on toothpicks being distributed by a woman named Donna who remembers you from last week. You can only go down one aisle. The other aisle's samples will be gone by the time you loop back. The stakes could not be lower but your heart rate suggests otherwise.

Donna sees you hesitate. Donna is disappointed. You have let Donna down.

retail Donna tiny sausages
Difficulty: Surprisingly high
legal

The Trolley Has a Lawyer

Before you can touch the lever, a man in a navy suit steps out from behind the trolley and hands you a card. He represents the trolley. The trolley, he explains, has been operating within its legal right-of-way. The five people on the track are trespassing. His client (the trolley) bears no liability. If you pull the lever, you are interfering with the lawful operation of a transit vehicle, and the trolley (his client) will pursue damages. He has a briefcase. Inside the briefcase is another, smaller briefcase.

The trolley's lawyer bills $750 an hour and this conversation has already lasted forty seconds.

liability trespassing billable hours
Difficulty: Litigious
emotional

The Trolley Is Fine. Everyone Is Fine. But No One Thanked You for Standing by the Lever All Day Just in Case

You stood by the lever for nine hours. In the rain. You brought a lunch. You watched every trolley pass safely. No one was ever in danger. But you were ready. You were so ready. And not one person said "hey, thanks for watching the lever." The trolley operators didn't wave. The pedestrians didn't acknowledge you. A child pointed at you and his mother said "don't stare." You are going home now. You are a little sad.

You will be back tomorrow. You don't know why. Neither does your therapist.

thankless vigilance rain
Difficulty: Emotional
philosophical endgame

There Are No Tracks. There Is No Trolley. There Is Only the Lever, and It Does Nothing, and You Pull It Anyway.

You are standing in a field. There is a lever. It is connected to nothing. There are no tracks. There is no trolley. There are no people. You pull the lever. It moves freely, with no resistance. Nothing happens. You pull it again. Nothing happens again. You will pull it a third time. You know nothing will happen. You pull it. You feel something. Not satisfaction. Not relief. Something else. Something you cannot name. You let go of the lever. You walk away. Halfway across the field, you turn back. The lever is still there. You think about it for the rest of your life.

This is either the most profound or the most pointless entry in this list, and the fact that you cannot tell which is, itself, the trolley problem.

absurdism Camus lever
Difficulty: Yes
